## The Biology of Hair Follicle Cycling

Hair does not grow continuously. Each follicle cycles through distinct phases that determine when and how much hair is produced.

**Anagen:** The growth phase. During anagen, the hair matrix cells at the base of the follicle divide rapidly, producing the hair shaft. Anagen duration determines hair length and, for scalp hair, typically lasts 2 to 6 years in healthy follicles.

**Catagen:** The transitional phase. The lower follicle regresses, the dermal papilla condenses, and hair shaft production stops. This phase lasts roughly 2 to 3 weeks.

**Telogen:** The resting phase. The follicle sits dormant, with the old hair shaft (club hair) retained and the stem cell population in the bulge region quiescent. Telogen lasts 3 to 4 months in healthy follicles.

**Exogen:** A sub-phase within or following telogen in which the club hair is shed. This is the visible shedding people notice.

**Anagen re-entry:** The critical transition where hair follicle stem cells (HFSCs) in the bulge region activate and initiate a new growth cycle.

In androgenetic alopecia, anagen phases shorten progressively with each cycle. Follicles spend more time in telogen relative to anagen. Over time, follicles miniaturise and eventually produce only vellus (fine, unpigmented) hairs. In advanced cases, follicles enter a permanently quiescent state where anagen re-entry does not occur even in the absence of ongoing DHT signalling.

The key target for any treatment seeking to reverse or slow this progression is the anagen re-entry transition.

## What Controls Anagen Re-Entry?

HFSC activation from the bulge region is regulated by multiple intersecting signals:

**Wnt/beta-catenin signalling:** A well-characterised pro-activation pathway. Beta-catenin accumulation in HFSCs promotes transition from quiescence to activation. Many experimental hair growth compounds have targeted this pathway.

**BMP signalling:** Bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs) promote HFSC quiescence. During the lead-up to anagen re-entry, BMP inhibition in the follicle environment is required for activation to proceed.

**FGF signalling:** Fibroblast growth factors, particularly FGF-7 and FGF-10 from dermal papilla cells, signal to HFSCs during anagen re-entry.

**Metabolic state:** This is the pathway that recent research on MPC inhibition has illuminated. HFSCs require a specific metabolic transition to move from quiescence to activation. Research has shown that HFSCs in the quiescent state rely heavily on oxidative phosphorylation via the TCA cycle. Activation correlates with a shift toward glycolytic and fatty acid oxidation-based metabolism.

This metabolic requirement is not secondary to the signalling pathways above; it is a parallel requirement that all other activation signals must be compatible with.

## MPC Inhibition and HFSC Activation

The mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC) is a transporter in the inner mitochondrial membrane that carries pyruvate from the cytoplasm into the mitochondrial matrix, feeding it into the TCA cycle. In quiescent HFSCs, high MPC activity supports the oxidative phosphorylation state that correlates with dormancy.

Research demonstrating this connection showed that pharmacological MPC inhibition in follicle cell cultures and in vivo models altered HFSC gene expression profiles toward activation-associated states. The mechanism is not by forcing cells to proliferate; it is by removing a metabolic condition that maintains quiescence.

JXL-069 is a selective MPC inhibitor. Its topical formulation is designed to deliver MPC inhibition at the follicular level without significant systemic uptake. The Phase 2a trial (NCT06393452) provides the first human data on whether topical MPC inhibition translates into observable hair density changes.

## The Ki67 Marker: What It Tells Us

Ki67 is a nuclear protein expressed in cells that are actively proliferating. It is absent in quiescent cells and expressed during all active phases of the cell cycle (G1, S, G2, and mitosis). In hair follicle research, Ki67 immunostaining is used to identify actively dividing cells in the follicle matrix and bulge region.

Increased Ki67 expression in the follicle bulge region is a direct marker of HFSC activation. When researchers apply a compound and observe increased Ki67 in the bulge, it is evidence that previously quiescent stem cells have entered an active proliferative state.

Published preclinical data supporting MPC inhibition as a mechanism for HFSC activation includes Ki67 staining showing increased proliferation in the follicle bulge following MPC inhibitor treatment compared to vehicle controls. This is mechanistic confirmation that the metabolic shift induced by MPC inhibition corresponds to actual stem cell activation, not simply altered gene expression.

The Ki67 data is important because it bridges the gap between the metabolic hypothesis and observed cell behaviour. A compound can produce interesting changes in gene expression that do not translate into actual cell proliferation. Ki67 positivity in the bulge means cells are actually dividing, which is the prerequisite for follicle cycling to restart.

## What Activation Means for Regrowth Timeline

HFSC activation is not equivalent to visible hair regrowth. The sequence from activation to visible hair shaft requires several biological steps:

1. HFSC activation and initial proliferation (weeks 1 to 3 after anagen re-entry initiation)
2. Follicle matrix cell expansion and dermal papilla induction (weeks 3 to 6)
3. Hair shaft production initiated (approximately week 6 to 8)
4. Hair shaft emergence through the scalp surface (approximately week 10 to 14, depending on growth rate)
5. Hair shaft long enough to be visually apparent (approximately week 16 to 24)

This timeline explains why researchers using JXL-069 based on the PP405 trial protocol do not expect visible results in the first 8 to 12 weeks. The biological processes between stem cell activation and a visibly thicker scalp require time that cannot be compressed.

It also explains why some researchers observe increased shedding in the early weeks of a protocol. Follicles transitioning from telogen to anagen first shed the retained club hair (exogen), which can create a temporary increase in shed hairs before new growth becomes visible.

## Comparison to Other Stem Cell Approaches

Several other approaches claim to target hair follicle stem cells. None have the same mechanistic specificity as MPC inhibition.

**PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma):** Concentrated growth factors derived from the patient's own blood are injected into the scalp. Growth factors in PRP (PDGF, VEGF, EGF) can stimulate cell proliferation broadly, but PRP does not specifically target HFSC quiescence mechanisms. Clinical results are inconsistent.

**Exosomes:** Extracellular vesicles derived from stem cell cultures contain signalling molecules that may influence follicle behaviour. Early clinical data is promising but lacks large controlled trials.

**Wnt activators:** Various topical compounds targeting the Wnt pathway (some peptides, some small molecules) aim to activate beta-catenin signalling in HFSCs. These target the signalling pathway rather than the metabolic state. They may be complementary to MPC inhibition.

**Adipose-derived stem cell injections:** Injecting fat-derived stem cells into the scalp to release paracrine signals. Results are variable. This approach targets a different mechanism and is not specific to HFSC activation.

The distinguishing feature of MPC inhibition is that it targets a metabolic bottleneck that is required for HFSC activation regardless of what signalling pathway the activation signal comes through. This positions it as potentially complementary to other approaches rather than competing.

## FAQ

**What is a hair follicle stem cell?**
Hair follicle stem cells (HFSCs) are adult stem cells located primarily in the bulge region of the follicle outer root sheath. They generate the transit-amplifying cells that produce the hair matrix and ultimately the hair shaft during each anagen phase.

**Does MPC inhibition force hair follicles to grow?**
No. MPC inhibition removes a metabolic barrier that maintains stem cell quiescence. It enables activation to occur but requires the follicle's normal signalling environment to support progression through anagen re-entry.

**What does the Ki67 data actually confirm?**
Ki67 positivity in the follicle bulge confirms that cells previously in a quiescent (non-dividing) state have entered active cell division, which is the biological definition of stem cell activation.

**How long does it take to see results from HFSC activation?**
Based on the timeline from stem cell activation to visible hair emergence, most researchers do not expect visible changes before week 16, with the primary endpoint in the PP405 trial assessed at 6 months.

**Is JXL-069 the only compound that activates HFSCs through MPC inhibition?**
JXL-069 is the most advanced MPC inhibitor to reach clinical trials for hair loss. Other MPC inhibitors exist in research contexts but have not been evaluated in hair follicle applications at the same level.
