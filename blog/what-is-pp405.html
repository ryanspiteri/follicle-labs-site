What is PP405? The Hair Loss Treatment Getting Serious Scientific Attention

Follicle Labs Research Library · April 2026

Hair loss research has produced two dominant treatments for decades: minoxidil and finasteride. Both have real limitations. Both leave a significant percentage of users with continued progression. For the estimated 50% of men affected by androgenetic alopecia by age 50, that is a frustrating reality.

PP405 is different. It does not dilate blood vessels. It does not block hormones. It targets something more fundamental: the stem cells inside the hair follicle itself.

This post explains what PP405 is, where it came from, what the clinical data shows, and why researchers and informed consumers are paying close attention.

What Is PP405?

PP405 is a small molecule compound developed by Pelage Pharmaceuticals in collaboration with researchers at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA). It belongs to a class called mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC) inhibitors.

The MPC is a protein complex on the inner mitochondrial membrane. It regulates how cells use pyruvate for energy. In hair follicle stem cells (HFSCs), MPC activity is closely linked to the stem cell's metabolic state. When MPC activity is too high, stem cells remain in a quiescent (dormant) state. They do not divide. They do not regenerate hair.

PP405 inhibits MPC activity. This shifts the metabolic state of HFSCs, pushing them from quiescence toward activation. Activated stem cells begin the hair growth cycle. Dormant follicles can re-enter the anagen (growth) phase.

This is a mechanistically distinct target from anything in the current treatment landscape.

Who Developed PP405?

Pelage Pharmaceuticals is a biotechnology company founded to commercialise research originating from UCLA. The scientific foundation was built by Dr. William Lowry and colleagues at UCLA's Department of Molecular, Cell and Developmental Biology.

Their research, published in peer-reviewed journals, identified that hair follicle stem cell activation is regulated by cellular metabolism. Specifically, they found that inhibiting mitochondrial pyruvate uptake could shift HFSCs out of quiescence and drive follicle regeneration.

Pelage licensed the technology and advanced it through preclinical studies before initiating human trials. The compound progressed through Phase 1 safety studies before entering Phase 2a efficacy trials.

This is not garage science. This is a university-originated, peer-reviewed research programme with institutional backing and a structured clinical development pathway.

The Phase 2a Clinical Trial: What the Data Shows

The Phase 2a trial of PP405 is registered as NCT06393452 on ClinicalTrials.gov. It enrolled 78 adults with androgenetic alopecia. Participants applied PP405 topically for four weeks, with a 12-week follow-up period.

The headline result: 31% of participants showed greater than 20% increase in hair density at week 8.

That figure is significant for several reasons. First, it occurred with only four weeks of active treatment. Second, the density increases were measured in areas previously considered non-responsive, including zones with significant follicle miniaturisation. Third, some participants demonstrated terminal hair growth in previously bald areas, not just density improvements in thinning zones.

Terminal hair growth in a bald area represents follicle reactivation. It is not the same as slowing loss. It is measurable regeneration.

The follow-up data also indicated that results persisted beyond the active treatment window. This suggests the mechanism produces durable changes in follicle biology rather than temporary stimulation.

Full Phase 3 trial details are expected to be announced as the programme advances. The Phase 2a data was sufficient to support continued development.

How PP405 Differs from Minoxidil and Finasteride

Understanding why PP405 is scientifically interesting requires understanding the limitations of existing treatments.

Minoxidil was originally developed as an oral antihypertensive. Its hair growth effect was discovered as a side effect. Topically applied, it causes vasodilation, increasing blood flow to the scalp and extending the anagen phase. It does not target follicle stem cells. It has no direct effect on the biological machinery that determines whether a dormant follicle reactivates. It works only on follicles that are still partially active. Once a follicle is fully dormant or the papilla has regressed significantly, minoxidil has no pathway to act on it.

Finasteride blocks the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). DHT binds to androgen receptors in the dermal papilla and progressively miniaturises the follicle. By reducing systemic DHT, finasteride slows this process. It does not regenerate follicles. It preserves existing ones. It also carries well-documented side effects related to systemic hormonal interference, including sexual dysfunction and, in some cases, persistent post-treatment effects now classified under post-finasteride syndrome.

PP405 operates upstream of both mechanisms. It does not need DHT to be present to work. It does not need blood flow to change. It targets the metabolic switch inside the stem cell itself. This means it can, in principle, act on follicles that are already dormant, not just those that are declining.

This is the key distinction. Minoxidil and finasteride are maintenance and slowing tools. PP405 is a reactivation tool.

Phase 3 trials represent the final stage before regulatory approval consideration. Based on the Phase 2a timeline and the typical drug development pathway, Phase 3 initiation is anticipated within the next two to three years pending regulatory feedback and funding milestones.

A successful Phase 3 outcome would place PP405 as the first fundamentally new class of hair loss treatment in over two decades. Approval in major markets (FDA, EMA, TGA) would follow its own timeline from there.

In the interim, the research community is not waiting. The mechanistic framework established by Pelage and UCLA has generated substantial academic interest. MPC inhibition as a hair follicle biology tool is now an active area of independent research globally.
