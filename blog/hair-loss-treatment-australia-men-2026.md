## The Australian Hair Loss Landscape

Androgenetic alopecia (AGA) affects approximately one in two Australian men by age 50. Population surveys suggest onset before age 30 is becoming more common, with some estimates placing early-onset AGA in around 16% of men aged 18 to 29.

The drivers of this trend are not fully characterised, but factors under investigation include:

**Stress and cortisol:** Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol levels, which can trigger telogen effluvium (diffuse shedding) and accelerate androgenetic progression. Australia's long work hours, high cost of living in major cities, and competitive professional culture create a high-stress population.

**Diet and metabolic factors:** Insulin resistance, low dietary antioxidants, and processed food consumption have associations with accelerated hair loss in several observational studies.

**Genetic predisposition:** Australian men of Western European descent carry higher rates of the androgen receptor gene variants associated with AGA than many other population groups.

**Environmental factors:** UV exposure, which is exceptionally high in Australia, produces oxidative stress in scalp tissue that may accelerate follicle miniaturisation.

The result is a large population of Australian men actively seeking effective treatment, many of whom are not finding satisfactory results with current options.

## Why Finasteride and Minoxidil Are Not Enough for Many Men

Finasteride and minoxidil remain the only TGA-approved pharmacological treatments for male pattern hair loss in Australia. Both have genuine efficacy in defined populations. Neither is sufficient for a large subset of Australian men who need more.

### Finasteride: Effective But Limited

Finasteride (1mg daily) reduces scalp DHT by approximately 70% and slows hair loss progression in roughly 80% of users. Approximately 30 to 40% of users see meaningful regrowth. These are solid numbers for a 30-year-old drug.

The limitations are significant:

**Non-responders:** Approximately 20% of finasteride users see no meaningful benefit. Their hair loss continues despite sustained DHT suppression. This likely reflects a population where HFSC quiescence, rather than ongoing DHT activity, is the primary driver.

**Plateau effect:** Many finasteride users maintain hair density for years but cannot recover hair already lost. The compound is better at holding ground than recapturing it.

**Side effects:** A subset of users (estimates range from 2% to 15% in different studies) report sexual side effects. A smaller proportion report persistent effects beyond discontinuation. For men in their twenties and thirties, this risk calculus is significant.

**Structural limitation:** Finasteride does not address dormant follicles. A follicle that has been miniaturised for years may require direct stem cell activation, not DHT reduction, to restart productive cycling.

### Minoxidil: Useful but Mechanistically Shallow

Minoxidil, applied topically or taken orally at low doses (0.25 to 2.5mg), remains widely used. Its mechanism is not fully characterised but involves potassium channel opening, vasodilation, and possible VEGF upregulation.

Minoxidil is genuinely useful for extending anagen in some follicles and reducing shedding. Its limitations include:

**Dependence:** Hair loss typically accelerates when minoxidil is discontinued, because it does not address the underlying cause.

**Variable response:** Response rates vary significantly by patient. Those with diffuse thinning tend to respond better than those with focal androgenetic loss.

**Systemic effects at higher doses:** Oral minoxidil can cause fluid retention, tachycardia, and hypertrichosis (unwanted body hair growth) at doses above the low range.

Neither finasteride nor minoxidil addresses the metabolic quiescence of hair follicle stem cells. This is the gap that emerging research is beginning to close.

## Emerging Treatments: What the Research Actually Shows in 2026

### JXL-069 (PP405): Mitochondrial Pyruvate Carrier Inhibition

JXL-069, developed by Riverfield Biosciences under the clinical designation PP405, is the most significant new mechanism to reach human clinical trials in the past decade. It targets the mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC), a transporter that influences the metabolic state of hair follicle stem cells.

The Phase 2a trial (NCT06393452) produced a 31% responder rate in a treatment-experienced population at 6 months. This is meaningful data. It is not a guaranteed outcome for every user, but it represents efficacy in a population that had already failed or plateaued with conventional treatments.

The mechanism is additive to existing treatments. MPC inhibition does not affect DHT. It does not interact with the pathways targeted by finasteride or minoxidil. Combining JXL-069 with existing protocols is theoretically sound and increasingly common among researchers.

### Low-Level Laser Therapy (LLLT)

Several devices have TGA clearance for hair loss. The evidence base for LLLT has strengthened in recent years, with meta-analyses showing modest but consistent improvement in hair density in AGA. LLLT is generally used as an adjunct rather than a standalone treatment.

### Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)

PRP injections remain popular in Australian clinics. The evidence base is mixed. Well-designed trials show moderate efficacy for some patients; others show no benefit over saline injections. PRP is most effective when follicles are still present but miniaturised, and least effective in areas of advanced alopecia.

### Oral Minoxidil (Low Dose)

The shift toward oral minoxidil at 0.25 to 2.5mg daily has been one of the more significant clinical developments in the past 3 years in Australia. Lower doses provide meaningful benefit for many patients with fewer systemic side effects than older high-dose protocols. It remains a DHT-independent vasodilatory approach, but at low doses is practical for long-term use.

## The Research-Backed Protocol for 2026

Based on the current evidence base, a rational approach for Australian men with androgenetic alopecia in 2026 looks like this:

**Tier 1 (foundation):** Finasteride 1mg daily, if androgen-driven miniaturisation is the primary mechanism. This addresses ongoing DHT activity and provides a reduced-androgen environment for other compounds.

**Tier 2 (stem cell activation):** JXL-069 topical application, once or twice daily, targeting HFSC metabolic quiescence directly. This addresses the mechanism that finasteride cannot reach.

**Tier 3 (adjunct):** Low-dose oral minoxidil (0.25 to 1mg daily) for vasodilatory support and anagen extension. Add LLLT if device access is available.

**Documentation:** Baseline photographs under consistent lighting, repeated every 8 weeks. Scalp dermatoscopy at baseline and 6-month mark to assess follicle density and shaft diameter changes.

**Timeline:** Commit to a minimum 24-week observation period before drawing conclusions. Early-termination decisions based on the first 8 to 12 weeks will produce misleading null results in potential responders.

This protocol addresses three distinct mechanisms simultaneously: DHT-driven miniaturisation (finasteride), stem cell metabolic quiescence (JXL-069), and follicular vasculature and anagen extension (oral minoxidil/LLLT). No single compound covers all three pathways.
